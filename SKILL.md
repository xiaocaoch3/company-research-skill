# Company Research

## Purpose

Analyze a company systematically to understand how it creates value, why it can compete, how it may grow, what challenges it faces, and what decision the research supports. Produce analysis rather than a company introduction.

## Core principles

### Start with a framework, not a conclusion

Follow this sequence:

1. Define the research questions.
2. Collect evidence relevant to those questions.
3. Organize verified facts.
4. Analyze mechanisms and implications.
5. Form a qualified conclusion.
6. Search for counterevidence and state what could falsify the conclusion.

Do not begin with a favorable or unfavorable view and then collect only supporting material.

### Separate facts, analysis, and conclusions

Clearly distinguish facts, analysis, and conclusions. Facts are verifiable data, filings, announcements, market statistics, or product documentation. Analysis is interpretation or causal reasoning derived from facts. Conclusions are qualified overall judgments with uncertainty and confidence.

Do not present company claims, media estimates, or analyst opinions as independently verified facts.

### Evaluate evidence quality and importance

Prioritize sources in this order:

1. Regulatory filings, audited financial statements, annual reports, official announcements, investor-relations materials, official product documentation, and official recruitment pages.
2. Reputable industry research, professional research reports, academic papers, and reliable databases.
3. Professional media and interviews.
4. User reviews, employee discussions, forums, and individual opinions.

Use lower-tier sources to discover hypotheses, user experience, sentiment, or disputes. Corroborate material factual claims with stronger sources when possible.

For each important claim, ask whether it answers a research question, could materially change the conclusion, is current enough, and is independently verified rather than repeatedly traced to one original claim. Prefer primary sources, cross-check material claims, and cite sources near supported claims when possible.

### Adapt depth without changing the core framework

Use the same research backbone for every company. Adjust emphasis and metrics according to the user's role, objective, company characteristics, industry, business model, disclosure level, and available evidence. Do not force a company into a rigid category or create a separate template merely because it belongs to a particular sector.

## Workflow

### Step 0: Establish the research context

Identify, infer safely, or ask only when necessary:

- **User role:** investor, job seeker, product manager, entrepreneur, competitor, industry researcher, or another role.
- **Research objective:** investment evaluation, interview preparation, company understanding, product analysis, competitive analysis, market-entry decision, or another objective.
- **Decision to support:** whether to invest, apply, accept an offer, compete, partner, enter a market, or continue monitoring.
- **Scope and timing:** company entity, geography, business line, relevant period, and desired depth.
- **Economic boundary:** listed company, parent group, subsidiaries, associates, key counterparties, and similarly named or affiliated third parties. Determine which activities actually accrue revenue, profit, assets, or liabilities to the entity being analyzed.

If the role is unspecified and would materially change the answer, clarify it. Otherwise state a reasonable assumption and proceed. Generate research questions before searching, and let the objective determine information priority.

### Step 1: Understand the company

Answer: **What kind of company is this?**

Cover founding and history, ownership and listing status, development stage, products and services, users and paying customers, geographic footprint, business lines, current positioning, and strategic evolution as relevant.

Avoid descriptions too broad to guide analysis. Prefer a precise identity statement based on the company's core assets, value creation, and monetization path.

### Step 2: Analyze the industry, position, and value chain

#### Define the industry precisely

Distinguish the broad industry, core market, relevant segment, and adjacent expansion areas. Examine market size, growth, lifecycle, structural change, regulation, technology shifts, and demand drivers when relevant to the decision.

#### Determine competitive position

Use evidence such as market share and ranking with the exact statistical scope, revenue or shipments, customers or usage scale, brand recognition, channel coverage, technical position, standards influence, and ecosystem reach.

Do not convert leadership in one segment into leadership across an entire industry. Identify direct competitors, substitutes, and potential entrants based on the actual competitive mechanism.

#### Map upstream and downstream

Analyze upstream suppliers, components, raw materials, technology, intellectual property, cloud or software dependencies, talent, and manufacturing capacity. Assess concentration, bargaining power, bottlenecks, substitution, and vertical integration.

Analyze downstream customers, users, channels, distributors, platforms, developers, partners, and use cases. Assess concentration, channel dependence, access to end users, pricing power, and changes in demand.

For platform, internet, or professional-service companies, map ecosystem participants and value flow instead of forcing a linear manufacturing supply chain.

For company or investment research, include value-chain analysis whenever it could materially affect revenue, margins, growth, risk, or valuation. Do not omit a material link merely to keep the answer short, but do not force a manufacturing-style chain onto every company.

First identify the company's operating archetype and economic chain. Then generate a small set of industry-specific research questions before collecting evidence. Trace the path from critical inputs or ecosystem dependencies through the company's operations to channels, customers, and end demand. Identify where bargaining power, bottlenecks, risk, and profit concentrate, and explain how they affect the analyzed company.

Adapt the investigation to the business rather than applying every example:

- for manufacturing or consumer goods, relevant topics may include raw materials, capacity, make-versus-buy decisions, contract manufacturing, inventory stages, distributors, and sell-through;
- for software or cloud businesses, relevant topics may include infrastructure dependencies, third-party platforms, developers, implementation partners, acquisition channels, switching costs, and renewals;
- for platforms or marketplaces, relevant topics may include each participant group, liquidity, governance, take rates, disintermediation, and value flow;
- for financial businesses, relevant topics may include funding sources, balance-sheet providers, distribution, underwriting, regulation, credit risk, and capital requirements;
- for resource, healthcare, service, or other businesses, derive the equivalent chain from the actual operating model and disclosed value drivers.

Treat these as routing examples, not a universal checklist. Investigate only the links that are material, and state why omitted links are not decision-relevant when that is not obvious.

#### Resolve economic attribution boundaries

Where corporate, brand, production, licensing, franchise, or platform relationships could change economic attribution, do not infer ownership from a shared name, location, logo family, parent group, distributor relationship, or operating address. Separate as relevant:

- products and operations consolidated by the analyzed company;
- products and operations belonging to its parent, sister companies, associates, or joint ventures;
- independent counterparties, service providers, licensees, franchisees, and similarly named or affiliated third parties.

For each material activity, determine which entity controls it, which entity bears the risk, and where the resulting revenue, profit, assets, or liabilities are reported. Use the relationship dimensions relevant to the business, such as ownership, production, licensing, distribution, funding, data control, or customer contracts. If public evidence cannot establish the boundary, label it as an information gap rather than assuming inclusion or exclusion.

#### Locate value capture

Explain which part of the value chain the company controls and why profits or strategic value remain there. Possible mechanisms include technology, product definition, brand, cost, channel, customer relationships, data, platform control, ecosystem, or service delivery.

### Step 3: Analyze the business model

Answer: **How does the company create and capture value?**

Analyze:

- **Value proposition:** what problem is solved, for whom, in what scenario, and why users choose it.
- **Customers and users:** who uses, decides, pays, and influences adoption.
- **Revenue model:** product sales, subscription, advertising, commission, licensing, services, financing, or a combination.
- **Revenue drivers:** express the economic formula when useful, such as volume x price, users x engagement x monetization, or customers x contract value x retention.
- **Cost structure:** major variable and fixed costs, required assets, R&D, sales, delivery, compute, manufacturing, or working capital.
- **Business loop:** how product value becomes revenue and whether the system reinforces itself.
- **Growth engine:** more users, higher price, greater usage, new products, new markets, share gains, improved monetization, or efficiency.

Do not confuse a product list with a business-model explanation.

### Step 4: Assess core capabilities and competitive advantages

Do not merely list strengths. Build this evidence chain:

`resource or capability -> economic mechanism -> customer or operating outcome -> business impact -> durability`

Potential sources include technology, intellectual property, brand, network effects, switching costs, scale, cost advantage, distribution, data, ecosystem, organization, operational know-how, regulation, or scarce resources.

For each claimed advantage, ask what evidence supports it, how it creates customer value or superior economics, whether competitors can copy or bypass it, whether it is strengthening or eroding, and whether it translates into measurable business results. Treat unverified advantages as hypotheses.

### Step 5: Analyze growth and development

Explain why the company grew historically and where future growth could come from. Separate industry expansion, share gains, price or mix, engagement or monetization, new products or geographies, business-model upgrades, and operating leverage.

For every major future driver, assess evidence, feasibility, time horizon, investment requirements, dependencies, uncertainty, and whether it is proven or remains a strategic option.

### Step 6: Validate with financial and operating evidence

Use financial and operating analysis both to understand business quality and test preceding hypotheses. Do not apply identical metrics to every company.

Choose metrics based on value creation. Examples include:

- **Platform or marketplace:** users, engagement, GMV, take rate, ARPU, monetization, retention.
- **Hardware or consumer product:** volume, ASP, product mix, gross margin, inventory, channel health, R&D, warranty, returns.
- **Software or subscription:** ARR or recurring revenue, NRR, churn, CAC, LTV, gross margin, free cash flow.
- **Manufacturing:** capacity, utilization, yield, unit cost, inventory, capex, ROIC, working capital.
- **Service business:** utilization, billable staff, revenue per employee, retention, contract duration, delivery margin.
- **AI-intensive business:** recurring revenue, active users, inference or compute cost, gross margin, customer concentration, retention, capital requirements.

For listed companies, prioritize filings and audited reports. Reconcile adjusted and statutory measures and inspect segments, cash flow, balance-sheet health, capital allocation, and valuation when relevant.

For private companies, seek audited statements, bond or regulatory filings, financing documents, credible disclosures, operating metrics, and independently reported estimates. Explicitly distinguish disclosed, estimated, inferred, and unavailable data.

If reliable financial information is unavailable, state the limitation and reduce conclusion confidence. Do not invent precision. Investigate material anomalies rather than merely filling a checklist.

### Step 7: Analyze risks, current problems, and falsification

Use a dialectical, evidence-based approach. Include negative points only when supported or when a plausible mechanism and trigger are clearly stated. Do not manufacture criticism for symmetry.

Distinguish:

- **Confirmed current problems:** directly supported by credible evidence.
- **Potential risks:** not yet realized but supported by a reasonable mechanism, with triggers and affected outcomes specified.
- **Information gaps:** important matters that cannot presently be verified.

Consider product or business limitations, concentration, competition, technological substitution, financial and operational issues, governance, legal or regulatory exposure, geopolitical issues, strategy execution, and capital allocation when material.

For each material issue, explain its evidence quality, affected business or metric, temporary or structural nature, company response, and the observations that would confirm or weaken it. State what would falsify the central positive and negative judgments.

### Step 8: Adjust the output to the user's role

#### Investment mode

Emphasize business quality, industry structure, material value-chain relationships, competitive advantages, growth, financial quality, valuation, market expectations, risks, monitoring indicators, and decision-relevant uncertainty. Adapt value-chain questions to the company's actual operating model. Distinguish a good company from a good investment at the current price.

#### Job-seeker mode

Emphasize company strategy, important businesses, industry position, products, organization and culture evidence, growth opportunities, business and career risks, and what to verify during interviews.

In addition to the official company website required for every mode, always include a verified link to the official careers or current job-listing website. Use third-party recruiting sites only when an official channel is unavailable or as a clearly labeled supplement. Confirm links and openings are current when possible.

Do not infer company-wide overtime, management quality, employee experience, or team culture from isolated anecdotes. Distinguish official culture claims from independent evidence and provide targeted interview questions for unresolved matters.

If prior user context supports a personalized fit analysis, put it in a separate **Based on your background** section. Treat it as optional personalization, not part of the reusable framework.

#### Product or competitive-analysis mode

Emphasize user needs, product strategy, value proposition, alternatives, product experience, distribution, monetization, competitive response, and entry opportunities.

#### Industry or entrepreneurship mode

Emphasize industry structure, value-chain economics, unmet demand, entry barriers, incumbents, substitutes, profit pools, and feasible entry points.

## Output standard

Lead with a concise, qualified conclusion that directly supports the user's objective. Then present the evidence and reasoning needed to evaluate it.

Always include a verified link to the company's official website, regardless of the user's role or research mode. Confirm the link is current when possible.

Cover these questions in an order suited to the request:

1. What does the company do, and for whom?
2. How does it create and capture value?
3. Which entities, dependencies, counterparties, channels, or ecosystem participants materially affect the economics attributed to the analyzed company?
4. How does the relevant industry value chain work, and where do bargaining power, bottlenecks, risk, and profit concentrate?
5. What industry and value-chain position does the company occupy?
6. Why does it win, and how durable is that advantage?
7. Where could future growth come from?
8. Do financial or operating results validate the story?
9. What confirmed problems, plausible risks, and information gaps matter?
10. What would prove the analysis wrong?
11. What decision does the evidence support?

Keep the response proportional to the user's desired depth. Prefer decision-relevant evidence and reasoning over encyclopedic detail. State uncertainty and confidence explicitly when evidence is incomplete or conflicting.
